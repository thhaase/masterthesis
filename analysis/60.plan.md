# Mechanisms of Alter Connectivity in Populist Ego Networks

## Research Question

Populist politicians' ego networks exhibit higher mean alter degree than those of non-populist politicians. Alters — users who commented beneath a politician's posts — are more connected *to each other* in populist ego networks. What social mechanism drives this?

## Core Argument

Populist discourse linguistically constructs an in-group ("the people") through people-centric and anti-elitist rhetoric. This discursive construction has a structural correlate: it facilitates actual group formation among the audience. The rhetoric does not merely mobilize individuals *toward* the politician — it connects them *to each other*. Communication produces social structure.

## Competing Hypotheses

### H1: In-Group Formation (Main Hypothesis)

Populist rhetoric produces community ties among the audience. Alters engage with each other as members of a shared group, developing sustained, supportive relationships across multiple threads.

**Predicted signatures:**

| Variable | Prediction | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| `weight` | Higher | Repeated interaction across threads |
| `component_count` | Lower | One cohesive group, not fragments |
| `prop_reciprocated` | Higher | Mutual, sustained engagement |

### H2: Provocation and Polarized Debate

Populist content is emotionally charged and divisive. Higher alter connectivity reflects *conflict* ties — users arguing with each other — not community.

**Predicted signatures:**

| Variable | Prediction | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| `weight` | Lower | One-off arguments, not sustained ties |
| `component_count` | Higher | Opposing camps (pro vs. anti) |
| `prop_reciprocated` | Ambiguous | Depends on whether arguments escalate |

### H3: Thread Depth (Null Mechanism)

Populist content generates longer, deeper reply chains. Alter-alter ties are a mechanical byproduct of thread structure, not a meaningful social process. Higher connectivity is an artifact of engagement intensity.

**Predicted signatures:**

| Variable | Prediction | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| `thread_size` | Larger in populist ego networks | More replies per post |
| `weight` | Low | Co-occurrence, not intentional connection |
| Populism effect after controlling for thread size | Null | Connectivity explained away |

## Available Variables

### Dyad Level (Edge Attributes on Alter–Alter Ties)

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| `weight` | Number of interactions between alter pair |
| `thread_size` | Size of the thread in which the tie occurred |

### Ego Level (Already Computed)

| Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| `populism_binary` | Whether ego's populism score > 0 |
| `component_count` | Number of components in alter subgraph |
| `prop_reciprocated` | Proportion of reciprocated dyads among alters |
| `ego_degree` | Ego's degree before removal |
| `n_alters` | Number of alters |

## Analysis Strategy

### Step 1: Rule Out the Null (H3)

Compare thread size distributions between populist and non-populist ego networks. Then test whether the populism → mean alter degree effect survives after controlling for thread size. If the effect disappears, the finding is about engagement mechanics, not social structure.

### Step 2: Characterize Tie Quality (H1 vs. H2)

Conditional on populism still predicting connectivity: compare edge weight, reciprocity, and fragmentation between populist and non-populist alter networks. High weight, low fragmentation, and high reciprocity support in-group formation (H1). Low weight and high fragmentation support provocation (H2).

## Results

### Step 1: Thread Size Does Not Explain the Effect

The null mechanism (H3) is rejected. Non-populist alter-alter edges actually occur in *larger* threads than populist ones (mean 533 vs. 374; Wilcoxon W = 234466, p < 0.001). The populism coefficient not only survives thread size controls but grows in magnitude across all three models: 0.23 (baseline) → 0.32 (+ thread size) → 0.52 (full model), remaining statistically significant throughout. Thread structure does not explain the higher alter connectivity in populist ego networks. The effect is social, not mechanical.

Note: 92 of 147 ego networks have no alter-alter edges and drop from M1/M2. The effective sample shifts from N = 145 to N = 55, which changes the estimand to ego networks with non-trivial alter subgraphs. This is appropriate for the research question — we cannot characterize tie formation in networks with no ties — but should be reported transparently.

> **M0 (Baseline, N = 145):** populism_binary β = 0.23, SE = 0.078, p = 0.003. R² = 0.059.
>
> **M1 (+ Thread Size, N = 55):** populism_binary β = 0.32, SE = 0.153, p = 0.041. mean_thread_size β ≈ 0, p = 0.936. R² = 0.078.
>
> **M2 (Full Model, N = 55):** populism_binary β = 0.52, SE = 0.157, p = 0.002. mean_thread_size β = 0.001, p = 0.004. ego_degree β = −0.001, p = 0.002. user_followers n.s. R² = 0.242.

### Step 2: Evidence for In-Group Formation

The pattern of tie quality indicators across populist and non-populist ego networks:

| Indicator | Non-Populist | Populist | Direction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mean edge weight | 2.73 | 1.49 | Lower in populist |
| Mean fragmentation | 0.969 | 0.856 | Lower in populist |
| Mean reciprocity | 0.002 | 0.022 | Higher in populist |

Edge weight is significantly lower in populist networks (Wilcoxon W = 237720, p < 0.001). Fragmentation is lower and reciprocity is approximately ten times higher.

**Interpretation.** Fragmentation and reciprocity clearly support H1 (in-group formation): populist alter networks are more cohesive (fewer isolated components relative to network size) and more mutual (alters reciprocate ties). Both are structural signatures of community rather than incidental co-occurrence.

The lower edge weight initially appears to contradict H1, which predicts sustained repeated interaction. However, the pattern is consistent with *diffuse* group formation rather than concentrated dyadic relationships. Populist networks have more alters and more edges, but the interaction is distributed broadly across many ties rather than concentrated in a few heavy-weight pairs. This is the structural signature of bonding social capital — many weak-but-real connections forming a community — rather than a small number of intense one-on-one exchanges.

Critically, lower weight does *not* support H2 (provocation) either. H2 predicts low weight alongside high fragmentation (opposing camps arguing). Instead, we observe low weight with low fragmentation and high reciprocity — a pattern incompatible with conflict-driven connectivity.

### Summary

| Hypothesis | Prediction | Observed | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| H3 (Thread Size) | Populism effect disappears with thread controls | Effect grows stronger | **Rejected** |
| H2 (Provocation) | Low weight, high fragmentation | Low weight, low fragmentation, high reciprocity | **Not supported** |
| H1 (In-Group Formation) | High weight, low fragmentation, high reciprocity | Low weight, low fragmentation, high reciprocity | **Partially supported** |

The overall evidence is most consistent with H1, with the qualification that the in-group formation mechanism operates through diffuse community ties rather than repeated dyadic interaction. Populist discourse constructs "the people" as a discursive category, and this construction has a structural correlate: the audience forms a cohesive, mutually engaged network — not through deep individual relationships, but through broad, distributed connectivity.
